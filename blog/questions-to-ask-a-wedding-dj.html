Questions to Ask a Wedding DJ Before You Book

Most couples meet with two or three DJs before booking. The problem is, without knowing what to ask, those conversations tend to feel the same — everyone says they're experienced, everyone says they'll work with your vision, and you leave without a clear way to tell them apart.

The questions below are designed to surface the things that actually separate a great wedding DJ from a mediocre one — things like what happens if equipment fails, how they read a room when something isn't working, and how they handle it when the timeline goes sideways.

Experience & Background

These questions establish whether a DJ has the experience to handle your wedding specifically — not just weddings in general.

  1. How long have you been DJing weddings? General DJ experience doesn't equal wedding experience. Weddings have a specific structure — ceremony, cocktail hour, dinner, reception — and handling the transitions between them takes time to learn. Look for at least 3–5 years of wedding-specific experience.
  2. How many weddings do you DJ per year? Volume matters. A DJ doing 5 weddings a year has far less real-world experience than one doing 50. There's no perfect number, but consistency over time tells you this is a primary focus, not a side gig.
  3. Have you worked at our venue before? A DJ who knows your venue already knows the load-in routes, the acoustics, the power access points, and the coordination team. It removes a category of unknowns from your wedding day. If they haven't worked there, ask if they'll do a site visit.
  4. Do you carry liability insurance? Most reputable venues require vendors to carry liability insurance. A DJ who doesn't have it may not be allowed in — and it's also a signal of professionalism. Ask for proof of coverage if you need it for your venue.
  5. Can you share reviews from recent weddings? Any experienced DJ should be able to point you to reviews without hesitation. If they can't or won't, that's a meaningful signal. Bonus: look specifically for reviews mentioning weddings at your venue or similar venues.

Music & Playlist

Music is the core of what a DJ does, but couples often don't dig deep enough into the process during consultations. These questions reveal how much control you'll actually have.

  1. How do we share our song preferences and do-not-play list? Good DJs have a clear, organized process for collecting your music preferences — an online planning portal, a detailed questionnaire, or a structured planning session. A vague answer here (like "just send me a text") is a warning sign.
  2. How many songs can we request, and how many are guaranteed to be played? There's a difference between a request list and a must-play list. Clarify which songs are guaranteed versus which are preferences the DJ will work in if possible. A good DJ will play everything on your must-play list and use your preferences to guide the rest.
  3. How do you handle guest song requests on the night? Do they take requests? Do they check with you first? Do they have a way to filter out songs on your do-not-play list? You should know exactly what will happen when your uncle requests "YMCA" for the fourth time.
  4. How do you read the room if a genre or song isn't landing? This question reveals experience more than almost any other. A great DJ adjusts in real time — reading energy on the dance floor and pivoting when something isn't working. Ask for a specific example of a time they had to adapt mid-reception.
  5. Will you learn the correct pronunciation of names for introductions? Mispronouncing the wedding party's names — or worse, the couple's names — during introductions is a moment that can't be undone. A detail-oriented DJ will ask for phonetic spellings in advance and practice them.
  6. Do you mix music live or play pre-made sets? Live mixing allows a DJ to respond to the room in real time. Pre-made sets are rigid. Neither is automatically wrong, but you should know which you're getting and whether it matches what you want.

On the do-not-play list: Be specific and don't be embarrassed about it. If there's a song that will clear your dance floor, cause family drama, or that you simply can't stand, put it on the list. A professional DJ won't question it.

Equipment & Setup

Equipment questions matter more than most couples realize — especially at venues with unusual acoustics, outdoor spaces, or limited power access.

  1. What equipment do you use, and do you bring backups? Professional DJs bring backup speakers, a backup laptop or controller, and backup cables. Equipment failures happen — the question is whether the DJ is prepared for them. No backup equipment is a dealbreaker.
  2. How much space do you need for your setup? DJ setups range from compact single-table rigs to full 10-foot-wide configurations. Know the answer before you finalize your floor plan with the venue — especially in tighter indoor spaces.
  3. Do you provide uplighting or other lighting? Many DJs offer uplighting as an add-on. If ambiance lighting is important to your reception look, ask what's available and whether it's included or priced separately. Having lighting and sound from the same vendor simplifies coordination.
  4. Do you have wireless microphones for toasts and speeches? Toasts require a handheld or lapel microphone. Confirm it's part of the setup — and ask if it can reach the tables where your speakers will be standing.
  5. What are your power requirements? Outdoor setups and historic venues sometimes have limited power access. A prepared DJ will ask about this in advance. If they haven't raised it by the consultation, bring it up — especially if your venue has any power limitations.

Day-of Logistics

These questions cover what actually happens on the wedding day — coordination, timing, and contingency planning.

  1. When do you arrive to set up, and how long does setup take? Most DJs need 60 to 90 minutes for setup and soundcheck before guests arrive. Confirm this fits within your venue's load-in window — especially if the venue has a tight changeover between a ceremony and reception.
  2. How do you coordinate with the venue, caterer, and photographer on the day? A reception runs on timing — dinner service, toasts, first dances, cake cutting. A great DJ proactively coordinates with the other vendors so transitions are smooth rather than waiting to be told what comes next.
  3. What do you wear? Sounds minor, but worth asking. Most weddings expect vendors in professional attire. Confirm the dress code matches your event.
  4. Do you serve as MC for the reception? Confirm whether the DJ handles all announcements — grand entrance, first dance, parent dances, toasts, bouquet toss, last dance — or whether you need to designate someone else to run the microphone. At Footloose Entertainment, DJ and MC services are always integrated.
  5. How do you handle it if the reception runs behind schedule? Receptions almost always run at least a little late. Ask how the DJ adapts — whether they have a process for compressing the timeline without losing important moments, and how they communicate with you and the venue if the timeline shifts significantly.
  6. What is your plan if you're sick or have an emergency on the wedding day? This is non-negotiable to ask. Every professional DJ should have a clear backup plan — a network of trusted colleagues who can cover in an emergency. If the answer is "that won't happen," walk away.

Pricing & Contracts

These questions protect you financially and make sure everyone's expectations are aligned before anything is signed.

  1. What exactly is included in the price? Get a clear breakdown: hours of coverage, setup and breakdown time, MC services, lighting, any planning tools or consultations included. What sounds like a complete package sometimes has significant add-ons that inflate the final cost.
  2. What is your cancellation and rescheduling policy? Ask what happens if you need to cancel or move the date — and ask what happens if the DJ cancels. Both scenarios should be covered in the contract with clear terms.
  3. Are there overtime fees if the reception runs long? Know the hourly overtime rate before you sign. If dinner runs 45 minutes late and you want the dancing to go until midnight, knowing the cost in advance prevents awkward conversations at the end of the night.

Red Flags to Watch For

Some answers — or non-answers — during a DJ consultation should give you serious pause regardless of how much you like the person.

  • They have no backup equipment. Equipment failures are rare but real. A DJ with no backup plan is leaving your reception in a genuinely vulnerable position.
  • They have no backup plan for illness or emergency. If their answer is "that won't happen" or "I've never missed an event," press harder. Every professional should have a contingency.
  • They haven't asked you anything about your wedding. A consultation that's entirely about the DJ talking and not about your event is a bad sign. A great DJ is curious about your venue, your vision, and your guests.
  • The price is significantly below market with vague terms. Unusually low quotes often reflect inexperience, minimal equipment, or an underprepared vendor. Price alone isn't a red flag, but price combined with vague answers is.
  • They have no recent reviews to point to. Experience is easy to claim. Reviews from recent couples are harder to fake.
