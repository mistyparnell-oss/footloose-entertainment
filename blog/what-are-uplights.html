What Are Uplights? A Guide to Wedding & Event Uplighting

Uplighting is one of the most common questions couples ask us — and one of the easiest upgrades to understand once someone actually explains it.

If you've been looking at wedding photos wondering what is uplighting — and what are uplights at a wedding that make certain venues look so warm, rich, and intentionally lit — there's your answer. It's one of those details that's hard to define until you see it, and then impossible to unsee once you know what it is.

This guide covers everything: what uplighting is, how it works, how to choose a color, where the fixtures go, what it typically costs in Jacksonville and St. Augustine, and whether it's worth adding to your wedding or event.

What Uplights Actually Are

Uplights — also called uplighting fixtures or wash lights — are LED lighting units placed on the floor around the perimeter of a room. They point upward, washing vertical surfaces (walls, columns, drapery, architectural features) in colored or white light from floor to ceiling.

The effect is a room that glows. Instead of overhead fixtures casting light downward, uplights fill the room's perimeter with light from below, creating atmosphere and warmth that standard venue lighting simply can't replicate. In photographs, the difference between a room with uplighting and a room without it is usually immediately obvious.

Most uplighting used at weddings and events today uses LED technology — specifically RGBW or RGBA LEDs that can mix any color across the full spectrum. This replaced the older approach of using colored gel filters over incandescent bulbs, which produced heat, burned out faster, and offered much less color flexibility.

How They Work

A typical uplighting setup involves wireless battery-powered fixtures placed around the room's edges — usually spaced 6 to 10 feet apart along the walls, and clustered around key focal points like the head table, ceremony arch, cake table, or photo booth area.

Modern wireless uplights communicate via radio frequency or DMX wireless signal, meaning there are no cables running across the floor. A single controller — usually a tablet or laptop running lighting software — can change the color, intensity, and behavior of every fixture in the room simultaneously. This is how a reception can transition from soft blush during cocktail hour to deep blue during the first dance, then snap back to your primary accent color for dancing — all without anyone touching individual fixtures.

  • LED fixtures produce negligible heat — safe to place near drapery, florals, and decorations
  • Battery-powered units eliminate tripping hazards from cable runs
  • Full RGBW color mixing allows precise matching to any wedding palette
  • Brightness and color can be adjusted live throughout the event
  • Most fixtures run 8 to 12 hours on a charge — plenty for a full wedding day

Uplighting vs. "special effects" lighting: Uplighting is ambient — it fills a room with color. Special effects lighting (moving heads, gobos, laser effects, fog-enhanced beams) creates movement and drama on the dance floor. Both can be used at the same event, but they serve different purposes. Uplighting is the base layer; effects lighting is the punctuation.

Choosing a Color

Color is where most couples spend the most time thinking, and it's genuinely worth it — the uplight color you choose will define the room's visual identity in every photo taken that night.

The most common approach is to match or complement your primary wedding color. Blush wedding? Warm rose uplighting. Navy and gold? Deep royal blue or amber. All-white? Warm white or champagne wash. That said, the relationship between your color on a swatch and your color projected onto a wall isn't always a direct translation — a few things to know before you commit:

Blush / Rose Warm and romantic, photographs naturally. The most popular choice for Florida weddings.
Warm White / Amber Universally flattering, works with any palette. Ideal when you want elegance without committed color.
Purple / Lavender Rich and dramatic. Deep purple reads as luxury; lavender reads as soft and romantic.
Blue / Navy Cool and sophisticated. Works beautifully in white or ivory venue spaces. Can affect skin tones in photos.
Green / Sage Less common but striking in garden-inspired or outdoor venue settings. Pairs well with wood tones.
Cool White Clean and modern. More clinical than warm white — works best in contemporary venues with high contrast.

A practical tip: ask your lighting vendor to show you the color projected on a neutral surface before you commit — ideally in a room similar to your venue. Colors look very different on a white wall versus exposed brick versus draped fabric.

Many couples also opt for dynamic color — the uplights shift from one color to another at preset intervals or at key moments in the reception. Common approaches: blush for cocktail hour and dinner, transitioning to a more dramatic hue for the dance floor portion of the night.

Where Uplights Go

Placement is as important as color. Even the right color looks wrong if the fixtures are poorly spaced or aimed at surfaces that don't reflect well.

Along the perimeter walls

The most common placement — fixtures spaced evenly along all four walls of the reception space, usually 6 to 10 feet apart depending on ceiling height and desired intensity. This creates a uniform color wash that envelops the entire room. Spacing too far apart leaves dark gaps between lit areas; too close together creates hot spots.

Focal points and features

Columns, archways, exposed beams, fireplaces, and built-in architectural details all benefit from targeted uplighting. A cluster of two or three fixtures aimed at a specific feature — the ceremony arch, the head table backdrop, the cake table — draws the eye and photographs beautifully.

Outdoor ceremonies

For outdoor settings, uplights are commonly used to wash trees flanking the aisle, the ceremony arch or arbor, and the sides of tent structures. Without enclosed walls to reflect light, outdoor uplighting is more targeted and less ambient than indoor — but it can be stunning, especially at twilight or evening ceremonies.

At venues we regularly work with in Jacksonville and St. Augustine — like Epping Forest Yacht Club, The White Room, and Club Continental — we've learned which walls photograph best, which architectural features are worth highlighting, and how many fixtures each space needs to avoid dark patches. That venue familiarity is part of what makes the setup faster and the result more consistent.

Are Uplights Worth It?

The honest answer: it depends on your priorities and your venue.

Uplighting is most worth it when the venue is a neutral canvas — white or cream walls, standard ballroom finishes, tent panels — where there's no existing architectural character to speak of. In these spaces, uplighting is often what transforms a generic hotel ballroom into something that looks like your wedding specifically.

It's less transformative (though still beautiful) in venues with strong existing character — exposed brick, dark wood paneling, stone walls — where the architecture already provides visual interest. In those spaces, uplighting enhances without needing to do the heavy lifting.

The other consideration is photography. Uplighting is one of the single most impactful things you can add if great reception photos are a priority. The difference between reception photos taken in a room with uplighting and without is significant and immediate. If you're investing in a good photographer, uplighting is a natural complement to that investment.

  • Worth it if: your venue has neutral walls and standard ballroom lighting
  • Worth it if: great reception photos are important to you
  • Worth it if: you want the room to feel distinctly themed to your color palette
  • Less critical if: your venue has strong existing architectural character
  • Less critical if: you're working with a tight budget and need to prioritize other elements

What Uplighting Costs

Uplighting is almost always priced per fixture, with a minimum quantity for the package. Pricing varies by market, equipment quality, and whether you're renting fixtures on their own or bundling with DJ or photobooth services.

Standard 12–16 fixtures Most mid-size reception spaces, 100–200 guests $400–$650
Full venue 20+ fixtures Large ballrooms, high ceilings, 200+ guests $650–$1,000+
Add-on (with DJ) varies Bundled with DJ package — usually the best per-unit value $200–$500 added to DJ pricing

Ask about dynamic color programming: Some vendors charge extra to program color transitions during your event (e.g., shifting from blush during dinner to deep blue for dancing). Confirm whether static or dynamic color is included in the quoted price before signing.

Uplighting and bistro string lights (also called café lights or Edison string lights) do very different things. Uplighting washes vertical surfaces — walls, columns, drapery — in color from the floor up. Bistro lights hang overhead and create ambient warmth above the room. Many couples use both together: uplighting defines the room's perimeter color and bistro lights add a canopy glow overhead. They're complementary, not interchangeable.

Uplighting at a Glance

  • LED fixtures placed on the floor around the room's perimeter
  • Point upward to wash walls and columns in color
  • Wireless and battery-powered — no cables across the floor
  • Color changeable live throughout the event
  • Typical quantity: 8–20 fixtures per reception space
  • Most common colors: blush, warm white, amber, purple
